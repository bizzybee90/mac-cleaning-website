Pure Water Window Cleaning Explained: Why We Don't Use Chemicals

20 March 2026 · 5 min read

If you've ever watched us clean your windows and thought "hang on, that's just water" — you're almost right. It is just water. But it's not the water that comes out of your kitchen tap. The difference is everything, and it's the reason your glass stays cleaner for weeks instead of days.

What Is Pure Water Window Cleaning?

We take ordinary tap water and run it through a multi-stage filtration system before it goes anywhere near your windows. First it passes through sediment filters to catch particles, then carbon filters to remove chlorine and organic compounds, then through a reverse osmosis membrane that strips out the vast majority of dissolved minerals. Finally, the water goes through a DI (deionisation) resin tank that polishes out whatever's left.

The end result is water with 0 parts per million (ppm) of total dissolved solids. To put that in context, Luton tap water typically reads somewhere between 280 and 350 ppm — that's because of the chalk aquifer that sits under Bedfordshire. All those dissolved minerals are what leave white spots and streaks when water dries on glass.

Strip those minerals out and you get water that actively wants to absorb dirt. It's essentially a natural solvent. When pure water meets a dirty window, it pulls the grime off the glass far more effectively than soapy water ever could — and it leaves absolutely nothing behind when it dries.

How the Water-Fed Pole System Works

The practical setup is straightforward. Our van carries a tank of purified water, connected by hose to a telescopic carbon fibre pole with a brush head and pencil jets at the top. The poles extend up to around 45 feet, which comfortably covers most 3- to 4-storey properties.

Water is pumped up through the pole and sprayed onto the glass through the jets while the brush agitates the dirt. We clean the glass, frames, sills, and rubber seals all in one pass — the whole window unit, not just the panes. Then we give the glass a final rinse with pure water and move on.

The entire thing is done from your driveway or garden path. No ladders needed for the vast majority of residential properties, which is safer for us and means less disruption for you. No one trampling through flower beds or leaning aluminium ladders against your render.

Why It Beats Traditional Methods

Traditional window cleaning means a bucket of warm water with washing-up liquid, a squeegee, and a ladder. It's how windows were cleaned for decades, and it works — but only briefly.

The problem is soap. Even when a squeegee removes most of the soapy water, it leaves behind an invisible sticky residue on the glass. That residue is a magnet for airborne dust and pollution. Your windows look brilliant for a day or two, then start picking up grime faster than they would have if you'd left them alone.

Pure water window cleaning leaves zero residue. Nothing sticky, nothing for dust to cling to. Glass dries completely mineral-free and stays visibly cleaner for much longer between cleans.

There are other advantages too:

  • No chemicals — nothing dripping onto your plants, paintwork, or anywhere near pets
  • No ladders — we can safely reach windows that are genuinely dangerous to access the old way
  • Frames and sills cleaned as standard — traditional cleaners often skip these because working on a ladder makes it awkward. With a water-fed pole, cleaning the full window unit is just part of the process
  • Better results on textured glass — pure water flushes dirt out of patterned or obscured glass that a squeegee can't get into

Why This Matters in Bedfordshire Specifically

Bedfordshire has some of the hardest water in England. The chalk aquifer that runs under the Chilterns loads our tap water with calcium and magnesium carbonates. If you've ever noticed limescale building up in your kettle after a week, that's the same stuff that would be left on your windows if someone cleaned them with untreated local water.

Our purification system removes all of it. The water that touches your glass is purer than bottled water — genuinely 0 ppm. That's not marketing, it's what our TDS meter reads every morning before we start a round.

There's another local factor worth mentioning. Luton sits right next to the M1 and directly under the flight path for Luton Airport. That means a constant low-level dusting of fine particulate — brake dust, tyre rubber, jet exhaust. On its own, that dust washes off easily enough. But if it bonds to soap residue left by traditional cleaning, it sticks hard and builds up fast. Remove the residue from the equation and rain just sheets off, taking the dust with it.

Common Questions

Does pure water really dry without streaks?

Yes. Because there are no dissolved minerals and no soap, there's simply nothing to leave a mark. One thing that catches people out: the glass can look a bit streaky while it's still wet. That's completely normal. Give it 5 to 10 minutes to dry and you'll see the difference — completely clear, no residue, no water spots.

What about when it rains?

This is the question we get most often, and the answer surprises people. Rain is relatively pure water — it doesn't make clean windows dirty. What makes windows dirty is dust and pollution sticking to residue that's already on the glass. If there's no residue (because we cleaned with pure water, not soap), rain simply runs off. Your windows will actually look better after rain than they would have with traditional cleaning.

Can you reach my upstairs windows?

Our poles reach up to 45 feet, which covers most 3-storey properties without any trouble at all. For a standard 2-storey semi or detached house, we're well within range. On the rare occasion a property needs something beyond pole reach, we have other access methods — but for 95% of homes across Luton and the surrounding areas, the water-fed pole handles everything from the ground.

Is it safe for conservatory roofs?

Absolutely. The soft bristle brush and low-pressure water are gentle on both glass and polycarbonate panels. We clean hundreds of conservatory roofs across Bedfordshire — the water-fed pole is actually the ideal tool for it because you get even coverage without anyone needing to walk on the roof or lean over it.
